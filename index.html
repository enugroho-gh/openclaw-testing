1940s – 1950s

The Dawn of Thinking Machines

In 1950, Alan Turing published "Computing Machinery and Intelligence", posing the question "Can machines think?" and introducing the famous Turing Test. Six years later, the Dartmouth Conference (1956) coined the very term "Artificial Intelligence" and launched the field as a formal discipline.

  • 1943 — McCulloch & Pitts create the first mathematical model of a neural network.
  • 1950 — Turing publishes his landmark paper and proposes the "Imitation Game."
  • 1956 — John McCarthy, Marvin Minsky, and colleagues host the Dartmouth Workshop — AI is born as a field.
  • 1958 — Frank Rosenblatt builds the Perceptron, the first trainable neural network hardware.
  • 1959 — Arthur Samuel coins the term "Machine Learning" while building a checkers program at IBM.
1960s

Early Optimism & Pioneers

The 1960s saw a burst of enthusiasm. Joseph Weizenbaum's ELIZA (1966) became the first chatbot, simulating a psychotherapist. Researchers built early expert systems and explored symbolic AI, fueled by government funding and grand predictions.

  • 1961 — Unimate, the first industrial robot, begins work at General Motors.
  • 1964 — STUDENT, an early natural language processing program, solves algebra word problems.
  • 1966 — ELIZA is created at MIT, becoming the first chatbot to pass a limited version of the Turing Test.
  • 1966 — The ALPAC report leads to a dramatic reduction in NLP research funding.
  • 1969 — Minsky & Papert publish Perceptrons, revealing fundamental limitations of single-layer networks.
1970s

The First AI Winter

Early promises fell short. The Lighthill Report (1973) in the UK was devastating, declaring AI had failed to achieve its "grandiose objectives." Funding dried up across the US and UK, plunging the field into its first AI Winter.

  • 1970 — SHRDLU demonstrates natural language understanding in a limited "blocks world."
  • 1973 — The Lighthill Report leads to dramatic funding cuts for AI research in the UK.
  • 1974–1980 — The First AI Winter: reduced funding, skepticism, and stalled progress.
  • 1976 — MYCIN expert system shows potential for medical diagnosis but never gets clinical adoption.
1980s

Expert Systems Boom & Bust

AI roared back. Expert systems became a billion-dollar industry, and Japan's ambitious Fifth Generation Computer project spurred worldwide investment. Yet the hype outpaced reality, and by decade's end, the second AI Winter set in as expert systems proved brittle and hard to maintain.

  • 1980 — XCON/R1, a commercial expert system for configuring computer orders, saves DEC $40 million/year.
  • 1982 — Japan launches the Fifth Generation Computer Systems project, igniting a global AI race.
  • 1986 — Geoffrey Hinton, David Rumelhart, and Ronald Williams popularize backpropagation, reviving neural networks.
  • 1987–1993 — The Second AI Winter: the expert systems market collapses, investment dries up again.
  • 1989 — Yann LeCun develops Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) for handwriting recognition.
1990s

Deep Blue & the Statistical Revolution

In 1997, IBM's Deep Blue defeated world chess champion Garry Kasparov — a watershed moment beamed into every living room. Meanwhile, statistical machine learning quietly replaced symbolic approaches, with Support Vector Machines and probabilistic models becoming the tools of choice.

  • 1995 — Random Forest algorithm and Support Vector Machines gain widespread adoption.
  • 1997 — Deep Blue beats Garry Kasparov in a six-game match, making headlines worldwide.
  • 1997 — Jürgen Schmidhuber & Sepp Hochreiter invent Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks.
  • 1998 — Amazon and others begin using collaborative filtering — an early form of recommendation AI.
  • 1999 — Sony releases AIBO, the first consumer-market AI robot pet.
2000s

Big Data & the Deep Learning Spark

The explosion of internet data and cheap GPUs laid the foundation for AI's comeback. The Netflix Prize (2006) popularized ML competitions, and Geoffrey Hinton's team showed that deep neural networks could be effectively trained, reigniting interest in neural approaches.

  • 2002 — iRobot releases Roomba — AI enters millions of homes.
  • 2004 — DARPA Grand Challenge for autonomous vehicles — no car finishes, but it sparks a revolution.
  • 2006 — Geoffrey Hinton publishes breakthrough work on deep belief networks, coining "deep learning."
  • 2006 — Netflix launches the Netflix Prize — a $1M challenge that supercharges collaborative filtering research.
  • 2009 — Google begins secretly developing self-driving cars (later Waymo).
2010s

Deep Learning Conquers the World

Everything changed in 2012 when AlexNet crushed the ImageNet competition, proving deep learning's power. In 2016, DeepMind's AlphaGo beat world champion Lee Sedol at Go — a game experts thought was decades away from computer mastery. The Transformer architecture (2017) set the stage for language models.

  • 2011 — IBM Watson wins Jeopardy!, showcasing natural language QA at scale.
  • 2012 — AlexNet wins ImageNet by a huge margin, launching the deep learning revolution.
  • 2014 — Ian Goodfellow invents Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs).
  • 2016 — AlphaGo defeats Lee Sedol 4–1 in Go, watched by 200 million people.
  • 2017 — Google publishes "Attention Is All You Need" — the Transformer is born.
  • 2018 — BERT and GPT mark the beginning of the large language model era.
2020s

The Generative AI Explosion

The 2020s became the era of generative AI. GPT-3 (2020) stunned the world with human-like text generation. DALL·E, Midjourney, and Stable Diffusion made AI art mainstream. Then in November 2022, ChatGPT reached 100 million users in two months — the fastest-growing app in history — ushering AI into everyday life.

  • 2020 — OpenAI releases GPT-3 with 175 billion parameters — a step-change in language capability.
  • 2020 — AlphaFold solves the 50-year-old protein folding problem, revolutionizing biology.
  • 2021 — GitHub Copilot brings AI pair programming to developers worldwide.
  • 2022 — DALL·E 2, Midjourney, and Stable Diffusion launch — AI-generated art goes viral.
  • 2022 — ChatGPT is released and becomes the fastest-growing consumer app ever.
  • 2023 — GPT-4 demonstrates multimodal reasoning; AI regulation debates intensify globally.
  • 2024–2025 — AI agents, video generation (Sora), and open-source LLMs reshape industries.
